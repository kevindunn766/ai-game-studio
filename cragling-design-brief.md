# CRAGLING — Design Brief v0.2
*(working title — original, no trademark conflicts, easy to rename later per studio precedent — see BATTLE BOA in snake-3d)*

**Status:** Milestone 1 (tap-to-follow + grip system + spline-limb climbing gait prototype) is built and has gone through several feedback passes beyond its original scope — see **Session Log & Current Status** below before doing anything else. Not yet approved to start Milestone 2 (biome bands).
**Genre:** Procedural vertical climbing, casual mobile
**Platform:** Mobile (Android first, matching studio pipeline), Godot 4.7
**Perspective:** Built as a third-person spring-arm chase camera directly behind the climber (reusing snake-3d/chimera-drift's proven `SpringArm3D` rig pattern), not the tilt-shift diorama look this section originally proposed — see Open Questions, this was a build-time judgment call, not a confirmed decision.
**Fantasy:** A tiny climber scales an ever-changing living mountain, from misty waterfalls at the base to a frozen, crystal-streaked summit.

## Session Log & Current Status (read this first on restart)

Milestone 1 is playable end to end: tap a point on the wall, the climber plans and executes a multi-step reach sequence to get there. What's built goes well beyond the original v0.1 scope, driven by hands-on feedback across several sessions:

- **Grip field** (`grip_field.gd`): true Poisson-disc placement (`min_distance=0.55`, `max_attempts=14000`, not the original grid+jitter), guaranteeing no two grips land closer than `min_distance`. A `crumble_chance=0.16` fraction are crumbling holds (`crumble_uses=2`) — the first obstacle type, ahead of schedule per the original Build Milestones order.
- **Limbs** (`limb.gd`): real 2-bone analytic IK (law of cosines), not a heuristic bend. Elbow/knee fold limits and wrist/ankle bend caps are derived from actual researched goniometry ranges (elbow min interior angle 30°, knee 40°, wrist bend cap 70°, ankle 40° — see the script's header comment for the full research summary and citations reasoning). Hands and feet exist as small terminal blocks that bend toward the grip surface within the wrist/ankle limit. Segment/hand orientation uses a hand-built stable look-basis (fallback chain across 3 axes), **not** `Quaternion(Vector3.UP, dir)` — that constructor is a documented Godot bug source (degenerate near-parallel/antiparallel to a fixed reference axis) and was silently breaking rendering for limb directions that swing through straight-down, which is routine for legs. Same fix applied in `waist_spline.gd`.
- **Joint-angle realism**: arms/legs bend toward the *front* of the figure (toward the wall), not away — real knee anatomy requires this (kneecap-side bend), and it was generalized to arms too per feedback. A shoulder/hip ROM cone (`_outside_joint_cone` in `climber.gd`) stops legs from ever reaching above shoulder height (real hip flexion tops out well below shoulder flexion) and replaced an earlier flat height-based hack.
- **Torso**: split into two boxes, Ribs (arms/head/hair/backpack) and Hips (legs), connected by a thick spline (`waist_spline.gd`). Ribs twist up to ±10° toward whichever arm most recently led (`RIB_TWIST_MAX_DEG`), Hips counter-rotate the opposite way at 0.6× that angle (`HIP_TWIST_RATIO`) — subtle by design per explicit instruction.
- **Push-pull body-offset cycle** (newest addition, in `climber.gd`'s `_process`): `BODY_OFFSET` is no longer constant. Whichever limb *role* (arm/leg) most recently landed sets a target wall-clearance the torso lerps toward — `PUSH_OFFSET=0.85` after a leg lands (body away from the wall), `PULL_OFFSET=0.45` after an arm lands (body in close), at `OFFSET_LERP_SPEED=3.0`. This is load-bearing, not cosmetic: it's the actual fix for knees clipping through the wall (the front-bend change above needs clearance, and this makes clearance largest exactly when the knee bend is most pronounced). It also means arms don't need a separate "let go/reconnect" system — the existing reach-distance clamp in the 2-bone IK does that for free once the anchor moves relative to a fixed grip.
- **Gait bias**: a diagonal contralateral preference (`DIAGONAL_BIAS=1.2` in `_plan_path`) softly favors moving the opposite-side limb from whichever moved last (right arm ↔ left leg), matching real climbing/walking gait — a nudge, not a hard rule.
- **Knee/elbow wall collision** (`limb.gd::_clamp_to_wall`): the push-pull cycle above reduces clipping but doesn't structurally prevent it, so every elbow/knee position is now also raycast-checked against the wall's real `CollisionShape3D` (same query technique as `tap_input.gd`'s tap raycast) each frame, and pulled back to just in front of the actual hit surface if the ideal bend point would pass through it. Deliberately a real physics query, not a hardcoded "assume flat plane at Z=0" shortcut, so it keeps working once walls are sculpted/non-flat. Confirmed firing correctly (not a no-op) via logged data during a real multi-click test: ~350 corrections across a short session, hit points landing exactly on the wall's Z=0 face, ideal (uncorrected) positions consistently behind it — i.e. real penetration was actually happening and is now being caught, on both arms and legs.
- **Knee/elbow self-collision** (`limb.gd::_resolve_body_avoidance` / `_resolve_sibling_separation`, wired up by `climber.gd::_wire_limb_collision_refs`): elbow/knee points now also push apart from each other (min separation `JOINT_SEPARATION_RADIUS=0.18`) and away from the torso boxes (Ribs/Hips approximated as bounding spheres, `RIBS_AVOID_RADIUS=0.23` / `HIPS_AVOID_RADIUS=0.21`) — deliberately cheap sphere-vs-point math (a handful of distance checks per limb per frame), not a physics query, per explicit instruction to keep this lightweight and scoped to just the joint points, not full-limb collision. Sibling separation reads each other limb's *previous frame's* elbow position rather than requiring Climber to orchestrate a same-frame two-pass update — a one-frame lag, imperceptible at 60fps, that avoids ordering complexity entirely. **Confirmed via logged data**: sibling separation fired 14 times in a short multi-click test with real corrections (up to 0.077 units) on both arms and legs — genuinely engaging, not a no-op. Body avoidance is correctly wired but essentially never fires under current proportions — elbow/knee points sit 0.55-1.0+ units from the torso centers even in tight/folded reaches, well outside the ~0.2 avoid radius, because the limb segments (0.7 each) are simply longer than the body is wide. Not a bug, just a dormant safety net for extreme cases rather than something that engages in normal play — worth knowing so it isn't mistaken for broken later.
- **Torso made taller**: Ribs box 0.5x0.3x0.3 → 0.5x0.34x0.3 (center Torso-y 0.2→0.24), Hips box 0.42x0.26x0.28 → 0.42x0.3x0.28 (center Torso-y -0.2→-0.24) in `Main.tscn` — both the box sizes and the gap between them grew a bit, so the waist spline reads slightly longer too. `LIMB_OFFSETS` in `climber.gd` (the planning-only anchor approximation) updated to match (arms y 0.22→0.26, legs y -0.28→-0.32) so planning doesn't silently drift out of sync with the real scene anchors.
- **Descent/rappel — a new, distinct movement mode from climbing** (`climber.gd::start_rappel_to`, `rope_spline.gd`): normal climbing is still structurally "up (or lateral) only" — `_plan_path` rejects any candidate grip lower than the limb's current one (`NO_DESCENT_TOLERANCE=0.05` slack) unless explicitly told otherwise. A tap that lands meaningfully below the climber (`DESCENT_TRIGGER_DROP=0.3` below centroid) triggers a rappel instead of a climb. **Reworked after the first version was flagged as wrong** — see the correction pass entry below; the rope and descent target no longer have anything to do with where the player tapped.
- **Gesture-based control scheme, four distinct entry points from one `tap_input.gd`**: single tap above = normal climb (unchanged); double tap above = **gap jump** (`Climber.start_gap_jump`); quick tap below = the single-arm rappel (`start_rappel_to`); press-and-hold below = **continuous rope slide** (`start_continuous_slide` / `stop_continuous_slide`). Above/below routing lives in `Climber.is_point_below()`, called *before* `TapInput` decides which gesture path to listen for. Single-tap-above is deliberately committed after a short deferred window (`DOUBLE_TAP_WINDOW=0.28s`) so a fast second tap can still be recognized as a double-tap instead of both actions firing.
- **Correction pass — rope/descent rework, gap jump widened, a real input bug found and fixed, knee midline guard added.** Feedback after the first descent build was blunt and specific: "You really trashed that decent mechanism. The rope shouldn't have anything to do with where the clicks/taps are. The rope should be perfectly vertical... Where is the jump upwards mechanic? Why do the arms and legs stretch? Make it so the knee joints don't cross over the climber's midline." Addressed each:
  - **Rope is now a fixed, perfectly vertical prop**, computed once per descent gesture, never redrawn toward a tap: top = whichever currently-held grip is highest, straight down (identical X/Z, no lean) to `ROPE_BOTTOM_Y=0.5`. `start_rappel_to` slides the anchor arm `RAPPEL_STEP_DOWN=1.1` down that fixed line (not to the tapped point); `start_continuous_slide` slides all 4 limbs' shared grab point down the same fixed line at `SLIDE_SPEED`. Tap/hold position now only decides *that* a descend gesture fired (`is_point_below`) — it never influences the rope's shape or the climber's resulting X/Z. **Confirmed via logged data across many independent triggers**, not a single lucky run: every `start_rappel_to` call logged identical anchor/bottom X and Z; every `stop_continuous_slide` logged `xz_match=true` between where the slide ended and where it started.
  - **Gap jump reach widened** from `MAX_REACH*1.05` (barely different from a careful climb, and could silently find nothing) to `MAX_REACH*1.5` — a real dyno-scale reach. Logged `reach_used` values repeatedly exceeded `MAX_REACH=1.26` (up to ~2.09), confirming it now genuinely reaches places normal climbing can't.
  - **Follow-up correction — the jump still just stretched an arm; the body itself never left the wall.** Widening reach alone wasn't a jump: the torso was still driven entirely by the passive push-pull lerp in `_process`, so the visible result was one limb extending while everything else stood still. Rebuilt `start_gap_jump` to physically launch the torso: a new `_jumping` flag suppresses the passive lerp, and `_apply_jump_progress` drives the torso through an explicit push-off-and-land arc via `create_tween().tween_method(...)` — position interpolates linearly toward the landing spot while a `sin(t*PI) * GAP_JUMP_LAUNCH_OFFSET` term adds an outward bulge along the wall normal, peaking at the arc's midpoint, so the body visibly leaves the wall and arcs back in rather than staying pinned to it. The landing limb's `reach_to` runs concurrently (kicked off unawaited, timed to match the body tween so both finish together), not sequentially after. **Confirmed with real logged data and a mid-flight screenshot** (temporarily slowed for testing, reverted after): the logged `arc_offset` traced a clean sine curve, 0 → exactly `1.4` at `t=0.5` → 0 at `t=1.0`, and the screenshot at the arc's peak showed the whole body pulled off the wall with both arms flung wide and a visibly larger ground shadow (same shadow-size-vs-wall-distance signal established earlier in this doc) — a genuinely different pose from "one arm stretched," not just a numbers-match.
  - **Second follow-up correction — too fast, and only one limb was relanding.** Two more explicit notes: the jump read as instant/twitchy, and the other three limbs stayed clamped to their old (now often far-away) holds instead of also letting go. Fixed both: `GAP_JUMP_DURATION` raised from `Limb.REACH_DURATION` (0.22s, same speed as an ordinary careful reach, which is why it felt twitchy) to a distinctly longer `0.45s`; `Limb.reach_to()` gained an optional `duration` parameter (defaults to `REACH_DURATION`) so the primary landing limb's hand tween can be retimed to match the slower arc instead of arriving early. Added a single-shot `_catch_up_limb()` for the 3 non-primary limbs, moving one only if its old hold was now farther than `MAX_REACH` from the body's new position.
  - **Third follow-up correction — still pointless, because that single-shot catch-up barely moved the body.** Root cause: centroid is an average of all 4 holds, so one limb jumping far only shifts it a quarter as much; the catch-up's "is my old hold still within `MAX_REACH` of the (barely-moved) centroid" check would routinely decide the other three limbs were fine and never move them, so a whole gap-jump gesture could net almost no real height gain despite one arm visibly flying to a distant grip. **Replaced `_catch_up_limb` entirely** with the same multi-step ladder planner normal climbing already uses (`_plan_path`/`_execute_path`), run for the 3 non-primary limbs against the actual tapped goal, right after the primary limb lands. Each step nudges the centroid a bit further, compounding into real climbed distance rather than one small adjustment. **Confirmed via logged centroid_y across three consecutive real jumps**: 1.45 → 2.71 → 4.31 → 6.13, i.e. 1.2-1.8 units of actual net height gained per gesture (vs. ~0.4 from the primary limb alone), with the catch-up ladder firing 4, 7, and 9 real steps each time — not a hollow animation, an actual climb.
  - **Fourth follow-up correction — the launch arc itself only ever pushed outward, never upward.** Sharp, direct feedback: "It only comes up off of the wall. Not up towards the sky." Root cause was literal: `_apply_jump_progress`'s sine bump was applied only along `launch_normal` (the wall-facing direction), so no matter how much real height the linear `start->end` travel covered, the *visible launch motion itself* read as "puffs toward the camera," never as "leaps upward" -- and since the camera is a SpringArm rig attached to the torso, a large outward-only push also pulled the whole camera back from the wall, compounding the "just pushed away" read. Added a second, independent sine term along world `UP` (`GAP_JUMP_VERTICAL_HOP=1.2`), summed with the existing outward term in `_apply_jump_progress`, so the arc now genuinely rises above the straight-line path in addition to pushing off the wall. **Confirmed via exact logged numbers**: temporarily instrumented `_apply_jump_progress` to log `linear_y` (what pure straight-line travel would give) against `actual_y` (post-hop) every frame -- `extra_rise` (their difference) climbed smoothly and peaked at exactly `1.20000004768372` at `t=0.5`, i.e. floating-point-exact match to `GAP_JUMP_VERTICAL_HOP`, then decayed back toward 0 by `t=1.0` -- not an approximation, the precise intended sine curve confirmed frame-by-frame.
  - **Found and fixed the actual cause of "arms and legs stretch" and the "trashed" feel**: the project has `pointing/emulate_touch_from_mouse=true`, and `tap_input.gd` was handling both `InputEventMouseButton` and its Godot-synthesized `InputEventScreenTouch` twin — every physical click was firing every gesture twice, and since gesture routing (above/below, climb/rappel) depends on the climber's *current* position, a doubled gesture could resolve differently the second time, cascading into repeated rappels/gap-jumps from one tap. Confirmed by logging `event.get_class()`: one physical click delivered a touch press+release *and* a mouse press+release. Fixed by handling only `InputEventScreenTouch` in `tap_input.gd`. Separately, the old rappel also had no distance limit on the anchor hand's `reach_to` target (it went straight to the tapped point's nearest grip, however far), which combined with the double-fire to produce genuinely unbounded-looking stretches — the rope rework above (fixed-distance `RAPPEL_STEP_DOWN` instead of an arbitrary tap-distance target) fixes this at the root, independent of the input bug.
  - **Knee midline guard added** (`limb.gd::_clamp_midline`, legs only, applied to the computed elbow/knee bend point right after the law-of-cosines solve): distinct from the existing `climber.gd::_crosses_body`, which only gates which *grip* a foot may target and says nothing about where the knee bends on the way there. `anchor_offset_x` (each leg's own anchor offset from the body's true center) is wired in from `climber.gd`'s `LIMB_OFFSETS` so the clamp can express the midline correctly in the limb's own local frame. **Confirmed firing constantly and holding exactly at the boundary** across an extended logged session — clamped X consistently equals the computed midline X to float precision whenever the natural IK solve would have crossed it.
  - **Correction to the above: retracted.** The repeated/drifting-position input seen during one windowed test session was mistakenly attributed to a phantom input source (e.g. a tablet driver) — the user has since confirmed that was them actually playing the game during that test, not an environment bug. Struck from the record as a false lead; no code or environment issue here.
  - **Fifth follow-up correction — the arc overshot again, and real forward progress got lost in the flourish.** `GAP_JUMP_LAUNCH_OFFSET=1.4` / `GAP_JUMP_VERTICAL_HOP=1.2` were fixed magnitudes regardless of how far the jump actually traveled, so for a short-to-medium jump (the common case) the bump dwarfed the real `start->end` travel — the body would puff out and up, hang, then snap back down near where it started, with barely any sense of moving toward the new hold. Replaced both fixed constants with `GAP_JUMP_LAUNCH_RATIO`/`GAP_JUMP_VERTICAL_RATIO` (0.35 each) applied to the jump's actual travel distance (`start_torso.distance_to(end_torso)`), clamped between `GAP_JUMP_MIN_BUMP=0.25` (so even a short jump still visibly leaves the wall) and `GAP_JUMP_MAX_BUMP=0.9` (so a very long jump doesn't balloon into an absurd swing) — the flourish is now always proportionate to real progress, never able to swamp it. **Confirmed via logged data**: a real jump with `travel_dist=0.46` produced `launch_bump=0.25` and `vertical_bump=0.25` — correctly floored at the minimum rather than the old fixed 1.4/1.2, exactly the intended behavior for a modest-distance jump.
  - **Sixth follow-up correction — the body's actual arc-target was still wrong, and the other 3 limbs stayed nailed to their old holds.** Two compounding bugs: (1) `end_torso` was computed from the average of all 4 holds after only the jumping limb's grip changed, so the body's real travel target only moved about a quarter as far as the arm actually reached -- the arm visibly outran the body during the arc. Fixed by anchoring `end_torso` directly to the jumping limb's landing grip (`best_grip["pos"] - LIMB_OFFSETS[best_limb] + landing_normal * landing_offset`) instead of the diluted centroid; confirmed via logged data that body travel distance went from roughly a quarter of the arm's reach to about 82% of it (1.87 vs. 2.28 units on one real jump). (2) Making the body travel that much farther then exposed a second, worse problem: the other 3 limbs' `current_hand_global` was never touched during the arc, so their hands stayed glued to old grip positions while the anchor (torso) swung a much bigger distance underneath them -- the very thing that looked like "stretching," just relocated to the limbs that weren't jumping. First attempted fix: glide all 3 toward a spot near the body's landing position -- wrong call, per the next entry.
  - **Seventh follow-up correction — gathering the other limbs in close was also wrong; the real fix is per-role posing plus a hard reach clamp.** Explicit direction: legs should dangle, arms should reach toward new handholds, and *no limb, ever, should render as stretched* — not "less stretched," never. Root cause of stretching in general: `Limb.reach_to()` drove the hand along its own independent tween while the body's arc moved on a completely different curve, so the true anchor-to-hand distance could exceed the arm's real length at various points regardless of where the two curves were individually aimed. Rebuilt per-limb flight behavior entirely in `_apply_jump_progress`, replacing both the old `reach_to` call and the "glide to a tucked spot" attempt:
    - The jumping limb and any other arm now reach toward a real target (the jumping limb's `best_grip`; other arms via a new `_flight_reach_target`, which finds a real nearby grip using the same `grips_within`/`nearest_grip` pattern used elsewhere) -- but every frame, the hand is placed at most `MAX_REACH` from that limb's *current* (moving) anchor, along the direction to the target. It visibly extends toward the hold and only actually arrives once the anchor is close enough on its own -- geometrically incapable of rendering a stretch beyond a normal careful-climbing reach, at any point in the flight.
    - Legs that aren't the one jumping don't reach for anything: they just hang `GAP_JUMP_DANGLE_LENGTH=0.9` straight down from their current anchor every frame -- a loose dangle, well inside natural reach by construction.
    **Confirmed visually**: a mid-flight screenshot (duration temporarily slowed for the check, reverted after) shows the legs tucked/hanging below the hips rather than reaching for anything, and the arms extended toward grips but visibly bent, not stick-straight or telescoping.

**Needs tuning next session** (explicit, not yet resolved):
- The whole rappel sequence has only been verified for correctness (each step fires, in the right order, with the right data) and basic visual presence, not for *feel* — is the single continuous arm-slide too fast/slow, does the leg-catch-up ladder look natural, is `DESCENT_TRIGGER_DROP=0.3` the right sensitivity for "the player pushed down" vs. an ordinary lower-but-lateral tap. Needs real playtesting.
- The regrasp-arm logic defaults to "closest reachable grip to the target," which can land above *or* below with no preference either way — worth checking whether that reads as natural or arbitrary once seen in play.
- Gesture timing constants (`DOUBLE_TAP_WINDOW=0.28s`, `HOLD_THRESHOLD=0.16s`, `SLIDE_SPEED=2.0`, `RAPPEL_STEP_DOWN=1.1`, `ROPE_BOTTOM_Y=0.5`) are first-pass numbers verified only for "does the right thing fire," not for feel. All need real thumbs on a real device eventually, not just desktop mouse timing.
- Gap jump has no "was this actually a gap" check — it'll happily fire even when a normal careful climb could've reached the same spot, since it just takes whichever candidate within `GAP_JUMP_REACH` is closest to the tap. `GAP_JUMP_REACH` was just widened from 1.05x to 1.5x `MAX_REACH`, which fixes "the jump doesn't feel different" but raises a new question worth watching in play: does the normal ladder-climb feel pointless now that the wider reach is so often better?
- The rope/descent rework is verified for correctness (rope geometry, reach distances, xz-match) via extensive logging, not yet for *feel* — is `RAPPEL_STEP_DOWN=1.1` per tap too big/small a step, does sliding down a rope that's offset from the climber's actual X/Z (whenever the highest grip isn't directly above center) look like a natural pull-toward-the-line or an odd snap. Needs eyes-on playtesting.
- `PUSH_OFFSET` / `PULL_OFFSET` / `OFFSET_LERP_SPEED` are first-pass numbers. Confirmed numerically that the offset oscillates correctly and confirmed via an unstaged visual side-effect (shadow size scaling with wall distance) that the torso really does move — but **not yet confirmed that the push-pull rhythm reads as climbing rather than just bobbing**. Needs eyes-on playtesting. (Wall-clipping itself is now separately handled by the direct collision clamp above, so the offset cycle no longer has to carry that job alone.)
- `WALL_CLEARANCE=0.08` (how far in front of the wall a clamped joint sits) is a first-pass number — untested for whether it reads as "resting against the rock" vs. "floating off it."
- Rib/hip twist direction (`RIB_TWIST_MAX_DEG` sign convention) was a best-guess and has not been confirmed to read as anatomically correct rather than backward — it's subtle (10°/6°) by design, which makes it hard to judge from a screenshot.
- The knee/elbow front-bias fix and the joint-ROM cones are mathematically verified (checked via logged world-space transform continuity, not just position math) but their *anatomical correctness as perceived* is still a human judgment call.
- **Hard-earned process lesson, worth remembering across sessions:** numerically checking that a control point moved to the right place is not the same as verifying the rendered result is correct — the Quaternion bug above passed exactly that kind of check while still being broken. Verify via the engine's own transform composition (`to_global()`, not hand-rolled local/global math) and actually look for NaN/discontinuity across logged frames, not just a single static screenshot.

## Governing Rules (Never Broken)

1. Design brief before code — this doc must be approved before any scene/script work starts.
2. Prototype first, no polish — grey-box primitives prove tap-to-follow, the grip system, and the spline-limb climbing gait feel good before any shader, particle, or final-art work happens.
3. Lock the feature list below once approved — no scope creep without an explicit new approval, same as every other project in this studio.
4. Polish (shaders, particles, SFX/music, biome dressing) is a separate phase, scheduled only after the grey-box prototype is fun to climb.
5. Playtest before export.
6. **Camera safety (hard-learned rule from snake-3d/chimera-drift):** the climber's visual root will be repositioned every step as limbs reach new grips. The camera rig must never be parented under any node that gets scaled/repositioned as part of that movement — camera framing is driven by code reading the climber's tracked position and adjusting rig distance/angle directly, never inherited "for free" from a parent transform.

## Core Loop

1. **Tap-to-follow movement:** the player taps a point on the mountain face within the climber's current reach; the climber automatically finds a path across nearby procedurally-seeded grip points toward that spot and climbs it, one limb-reach at a time, fully hands-free once the tap registers.
2. Each step, the nearest free hand or foot reaches for the next grip along the path — a spline-driven arc (elbow/knee bend, not a straight line) while the other three limbs hold their current position, giving an alternating bouldering gait rather than a walk cycle.
3. Gems are scattered along the direct route and in small detour pockets slightly off the critical path — rewarding a bit of exploration without punishing players who just climb straight up.
4. Cave mouths, found periodically, lead into short enclosed puzzle chambers. Solving one nets bonus gems. Caves are always optional and sign-posted — never mandatory to reach the summit.
5. Forced-obstacle stretches (crumbling holds, waterfall curtains, wind gusts, ice patches — see Obstacles below) gate progress at points and must be handled via tap-to-follow timing, not freeform movement.
6. Climbing upward moves the player through the mountain's biome bands (below) until they reach the summit — run/level complete.
7. **Climbing itself is up-and-lateral only — descent is a distinct mechanic, not the same tap-to-follow ladder run backward.** Tapping a point meaningfully below the climber triggers a rappel instead: the higher-held arm anchors a rope and its hand slides down to the target, the other arm detaches and re-grasps nearby, then the legs catch up (which is also what pushes the body out from the wall — see Character below). This wasn't in the original v0.1 plan; it was added because upward-only climbing needed *some* way back down, and a real rappel reads better for a climbing game than just running the same ladder logic in reverse.

This is the same overall shape as kindling's "one continuous world, escalating via bands, not separate maps" — reusing a proven pattern rather than inventing a new one.

## Character — the Cragling

- **Head:** small rectangular box, mounted on Ribs.
- **Hair:** a second rectangular "blob" box mounted on/behind the head, cosmetic-variant color.
- **Torso:** **built as two rectangular boxes, not one** — Ribs (upper, carries head/hair/backpack/arms) and Hips (lower, carries legs), linked by a thick spline (see below). This split was added specifically to support the ribs/hip counter-twist and wasn't in the original v0.1 plan.
- **Backpack:** rectangular box mounted on the Ribs' back.
- **Waist:** a thick, wide chain of box segments sampled along a Curve3D connecting Ribs-bottom to Hips-top — same spline-chain technique as the limbs, just wider/shorter and with no IK to solve.
- **Arms and legs:** each a real 2-bone analytic IK chain (law of cosines, not a heuristic bend), with fixed upper/lower segment lengths and elbow/knee fold limits derived from researched joint ROM (see Session Log above and `limb.gd`'s header for the full citations/reasoning). Segments are boxes sampled along a Curve3D between anchor → elbow/knee → hand/foot.
- **Hands and feet:** small terminal blocks at each limb's tip (not in the original plan — added per feedback), oriented by bending the forearm/shin's own direction toward the grip's surface normal, capped at a researched wrist/ankle ROM limit, so they read as pressing onto the hold rather than just continuing the arm's line.

**Climbing gait, per limb:** `holding` → `reaching` (spline animates over roughly 0.15–0.3s to the next grip) → `holding`, always one limb at a time (three points of contact). A soft diagonal-partner bias favors the gait alternating opposite-side limbs. The torso only translates once enough weight-bearing limbs are stable on new grips — mirrors real climbing weight-shift, and reuses the studio's established "logical state updates instantly, visual state interpolates" technique (see snake-3d's segment-follow fix) so the camera and body never jerk. **On top of that base motion**, the torso's *distance from the wall* now also cycles with the gait — see the push-pull body-offset entry in the Session Log above — and Ribs/Hips apply a small counter-twist toward whichever arm led most recently.

## Tap-to-Follow & Grip System

This is the single highest-risk, most novel system in the brief — the first thing to prototype standalone, before any biome content.

- **Grip generation:** the mountain-face mesh is seeded with grip points via a poisson-ish scatter that respects local surface normal/slope. Density is tuned per biome — sparse and far apart on ice (hard), dense on jungle vines (easy).
- **Reach query:** from the climber's four current limb anchors, only grips within a maximum stretch radius are reachable candidates.
- **Pathing:** tapping a point on the mountain projects it onto the mesh; a short greedy/graph search walks the grip network from the climber's current holds toward that point, and each step in that path becomes one limb-reach in sequence.
- **Forgiving fallback:** if the tapped point has no fully reachable path (too far, blocked by an unclimbed obstacle), snap to the nearest reachable grip in that general direction instead of rejecting the tap outright — keeps the casual, low-frustration feel the pitch calls for.
- **Controls grew from one gesture to four, all still built on this same raycast-a-point-and-route-it foundation** (see Session Log for the full build writeup): single tap above continues to be this exact system; double tap above adds a gap jump (a single wide-radius dynamic lunge, bypassing the walked ladder); a quick tap below triggers the single-arm rappel; a press-and-hold below triggers a continuous full-limb rope slide for as long as it's held. `TapInput` decides above-vs-below and tap-vs-hold-vs-double before ever calling into `Climber`, so `Climber` still just exposes one clear entry point per gesture rather than a single overloaded function branching internally.

## Biome Bands (base → summit)

One continuous procedurally-generated mountain, banded by height — not discrete levels or loading transitions, same technique as kindling's tier system and snake-3d's biome sequencing:

| Band | Character | Difficulty notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Waterfall Base | Wet rock, mist, waterfalls | Slick patches behind falling water; lowest difficulty, teaches the grip system |
| 2. Jungle | Vines, dense foliage | Vines double as usable grips; foliage visually obscures some grips (readability challenge, not a hard obstacle) |
| 3. Temperate Forest | Mossy rock, tree roots | Moderate baseline difficulty |
| 4. Desert | Sun-bleached sandstone | Crumbling holds become more common; grips sparser |
| 5. Crystal Caverns | Glowing crystal outcrops | Crystals double as grips; first cave-heavy band |
| 6. Rocky Highlands | Exposed cliff face | Wind gusts introduced |
| 7. Snowfield | Snow-dusted rock | Reduced stretch radius (gloves/cold, narratively) |
| 8. Icy Summit | Ice-slick rock, rarest grips | Hardest band; final obstacle gauntlet before the peak |

Exact difficulty tuning (grip density/spacing numbers per band) is a feel-tuning pass once the base prototype is playable, not a first-prototype blocker.

## Obstacles (introduced one at a time, per band)

- **Crumbling holds:** limited number of uses before they break away — forces route variety, can't camp one grip.
- **Waterfall curtains:** grips behind/under the curtain cycle visible/hidden on a timer.
- **Wind gusts** (Rocky Highlands onward): can push the climber off-target mid-reach; tap-to-follow must react/re-tap.
- **Ice patches** (Snowfield/Icy Summit): temporarily reduce stretch radius while standing on them.

Each is introduced in its own band and its own build milestone (see below) — no batching multiple new obstacle types into one pass, per the studio's "no scope creep" rule.

## Puzzles

Two flavors, matching the pitch's "some in caves, some tough obstacles":

- **Cave puzzles:** short, enclosed, optional chambers off the main route. First-pass ideas (not locked): grip-sequence memory challenges, weight-activated levers only reachable by climbing onto them, gem-gated doors. Kept minimal for v0.1 — the vertical slice ships with at most one puzzle type, expanded later once the core loop is proven.
- **Obstacle puzzles:** the forced-obstacle stretches above (crumbling holds, waterfall timing, wind, ice) are themselves the "tough obstacle" puzzle category — no separate system needed, they're a property of the climbing/grip system already.

---

## Gems & Fail State

- Gems are the primary collectible, placed along the direct route and in off-path detour pockets.
- **Casual, forgiving fail state (per the pitch's "real casual game"):** no hard death by default. A failed/mistimed reach has the climber hold their last stable grip or drop a short distance to the nearest secure ledge below — never an instant restart. This is a deliberate departure from snake-3d/kindling's harder fail states; flagged explicitly since it's a genre-appropriate call, not an oversight.
